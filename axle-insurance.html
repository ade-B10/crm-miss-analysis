What Is Consensus? Definitions & Thresholds

The CRM Miss Analyzer uses a specific, measurable definition of "consensus" to identify deals Base10 evaluated but did not invest in that later became breakout companies. Here are the three core criteria:

$25M+
Minimum Round Size
The company must have raised a subsequent round of $25M or more. This filters out companies that merely survived and isolates those that attracted significant capital conviction.
Top 30
Lead Investor Tier
The round must have been led by a top-30 venture firm. This ensures the company didn't just raise from anyone, it attracted the most competitive, signal-heavy capital.
Series A+
Minimum Stage
The round must be Series A or later, confirming the company achieved sufficient product-market fit and traction to clear institutional underwriting bars.

Why This Definition Matters

The threshold is intentionally conservative. We only count a miss if: (1) Base10 had a documented discussion, (2) there was an explicit pass or deprioritization decision, (3) the subsequent round was $25M+ from a top-30 firm, and (4) the timing confirms we could have invested at the point of discussion. This avoids inflating the miss count with companies we never seriously evaluated or that raised from non-signal investors.

The goal is learning, not blame. The question is always: "what market signal did we misread?" not "who made a bad call." By holding to a $25M+ / top-30 firm bar, we ensure every identified miss represents a genuine blind spot worth analyzing.

What the Team Might Be Missing

The Plaid analog could be tactical or strategic. Dani's key open question: "Does this need to be another Plaid, or can it get absorbed by voice/CX automation tools like Strada?" This is actually the right question, but it's unresolved. Plaid's path was independence + multi-stage expansion. If Axle's path is absorption by vertical tools, the dynamics change entirely.

This is a real question, but at $3M ARR with a clear use case (insurance carriers want automation), the scaling playbook is starting to emerge. Direct sales at this stage is the baseline.

The strategic fork: Is Axle the infrastructure layer that becomes the standard across insurance workflows (Plaid analog for a $5T+ insurance industry), or is it feature acquisition by vertical tools (Strada, voice automation platforms) that sell to carriers and insureds? The answer determines whether this is a $10B+ category expansion or a $500M-1B point solution. Team is asking the right question. But waiting for absolute clarity before moving could mean watching the entry price 2-3x.

B10 Miss Patterns That Predict Consensus

Analysis of Base10's historical misses reveals 7 recurring patterns. These are the internal reasoning patterns that, when present at the time of a pass decision, have historically preceded the company becoming consensus (raising $25M+ from a top-30 firm). These are not "bad decisions" - they are systematic blind spots that, when recognized in real-time, should trigger additional scrutiny before passing.

Pattern 1: "Category Is Sucky" / Static TAM Framing

HIGH PREDICTOR

What it looks like: Team sizes the TAM based on the company's current product and ICP, concludes the market is too small or the category isn't attractive enough. Fails to account for the company expanding into adjacent functionality and absorbing incumbent spend.

Historical examples: Companies building "point solutions" in seemingly niche categories that later became platforms. Plaid started as bank verification ($500M TAM at the time) and became financial infrastructure ($10B+).

Why it predicts consensus: Top-tier firms are often better at pattern-matching "wedge into platform" stories. They invest in the trajectory, not the current TAM. When B10 passes on TAM and a top-5 firm leads the next round, it's usually because they saw the platform play we sized as a wedge.

Consensus predictor strength:
85%

Pattern 2: "Fuzzy Despite Work"

HIGH PREDICTOR

What it looks like: Team does extensive diligence (market mapping, customer calls, comp analysis) but the conclusion is "it's still fuzzy." The work itself was rigorous but the framing prevents conviction from forming. Customer data is strong but gets discounted because the market narrative hasn't crystallized internally.

Why it predicts consensus: "Fuzzy" often means the company is creating a new category or is at the inflection point before narrative clarity. Top firms invest through ambiguity when the customer signal is clear. By the time it's not "fuzzy" to everyone, the price has 3-5x'd.

Consensus predictor strength:
80%

Pattern 3: Customer Signal Discounted by Market Thesis

HIGH PREDICTOR

What it looks like: Customer references come back exceptionally strong (8-10 NPS, hard ROI, willing to invest, unsolicited referrals) but the team discounts them because the market framing doesn't support conviction. The data says yes, the thesis says no. Thesis wins.

Why it predicts consensus: Customer love at the early stage is the single strongest predictor of breakout outcomes. When customer calls consistently return 8-9 NPS with affirming feedback and expansion signals, that signal should override TAM sizing concerns. Firms like Benchmark famously invest on customer signal alone.

Consensus predictor strength:
90%

Pattern 4: "Growth Is Too Recent" / Recency Discount

MEDIUM PREDICTOR

What it looks like: Company shows strong acceleration in last 3-6 months but team discounts it as "too early to tell" or "not enough data points to predict." Prefers to wait for another quarter of data.

Why it predicts consensus: Waiting for more data at the Series A is the most expensive form of de-risking. The price will 2-3x by the time the next quarter confirms what the current quarter already shows. The best Series A investors bet on the slope of the curve, not the length of the track record.

Consensus predictor strength:
70%

Pattern 5: Incumbent Response Over-Indexed

MEDIUM PREDICTOR

What it looks like: Team worries that incumbent platforms (insurance carriers, policy management systems) will build competitive functionality and eat the startup's lunch. Weighs theoretical incumbent response more heavily than the observed reality (insurance infrastructure has historically been slow to change, vendor lock-in is high).

Why it predicts consensus: In insurance specifically, incumbent response speed has been consistently slower than feared. The startups that became consensus in fintech were ones where banks could have responded but didn't move fast enough. Investors who waited for "proof the incumbent won't respond" missed the entry window.

Consensus predictor strength:
65%

Pattern 6: "Never Followed Up" / Lead Dropped

LOW PREDICTOR

What it looks like: Company was evaluated, flagged as interesting, but deal lead moved on to higher-priority deals. No explicit pass decision, just deprioritization. By the time someone circles back, the round is done or the price has moved.

Why it predicts consensus: Opportunity cost of the pipeline. When the team is capacity-constrained, the deals that get deprioritized are often ones that are "interesting but not urgent" which is exactly the profile of early-stage companies before they hit inflection.

Consensus predictor strength:
35%

Pattern 7: Pricing Anchoring / "We Can Wait"

MEDIUM PREDICTOR

What it looks like: Valuation at current round feels high relative to current metrics. Team assumes they can enter later at a better price, or that the company will need to raise again at a reasonable price if things slow down. "Let's track for the Series B."

Why it predicts consensus: Companies that become consensus never get cheaper. The $80-120M post that feels rich at $3M ARR becomes the $300-400M Series B at $10M+ ARR. The "we can wait" trade almost never works for breakout companies.

Consensus predictor strength:
60%
