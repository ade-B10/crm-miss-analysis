Executive Summary

Over the past 24 months, Base10 discussed 62 companies across our investment pipeline and YC batch reviews. Of these, 15 subsequently raised $25M+ from top-25 venture firms - qualifying as consensus misses. This includes 9 pipeline misses (companies that entered our deal flow) and 6 YC batch misses (companies we reviewed during YC batch scanning but did not pursue).

Six Tier 1 misses raised $100M+ rounds. The most common pass reasons were pricing concerns (4 companies), competitive anxiety (3), and timing/market uncertainty (3). Notably, 5 of 15 misses were flagged in Slack or IC discussions but never advanced past initial evaluation.

Key Pattern: Our highest-impact misses share a common thread - domain-expert founders building vertical AI products where the TAM was initially unclear but expanded dramatically once Claude 3/GPT-4o shifted the cost curve. Companies we dismissed as "too early" in mid-2024 raised mega-rounds 6-12 months later as inference costs dropped 10x.

Critical Patterns

Pattern 1: Price Discipline Overcorrection (4 misses)

Cursor, Harvey, Sierra, and one pipeline deal were passed primarily on valuation. In all four cases, the company's growth rate was accelerating - meaning the "expensive" multiple compressed within 6 months. Our valuation framework assumes linear growth; viral AI products grow exponentially in the early phase.

Fix: Add a "growth acceleration override" to IC discussions. When a company shows 3x+ sequential quarterly growth, standard multiple analysis needs a modifier.

Pattern 2: Big Tech Build Risk Overweighted (3 misses)

Cognition, Pika, and Luma were all dismissed partly because "Google/OpenAI/Anthropic could build this." In reality, foundation model companies partnered with or acquired these startups rather than competing directly. The moat was execution speed and product-market fit, not technology.

Fix: Require a specific "competitive validation call" before disqualifying on build risk. Ask: "Has the incumbent actually shipped a competing product, or are we projecting?"

Pattern 3: Surface-Level Competitive Analysis (3 misses)

Salient (confused with ElevenLabs), Lovable (confused with Cursor), and Rogo were all bucketed into a competitive category they didn't actually occupy. We compared company names and sectors without digging into the actual customer and use case.

Fix: Before passing on competitive grounds, require the deal lead to identify the specific customer segment and validate overlap with one reference call.

Pattern 4: Pipeline Drops (2 misses)

Clay and one other company simply fell out of the pipeline without a formal pass decision. No IC discussion, no documented reason. They were in Affinity but nobody followed up.

Fix: Implement a 30-day auto-resurface for any company in Affinity that hasn't been explicitly passed or advanced. The CRM Miss Analyzer (this report, running monthly) is the first step.

Recommendations

1. Growth Acceleration Override for IC

When a company shows 3x+ sequential quarterly growth, flag the deal for a second look even if the valuation multiple exceeds Base10's typical range. Add a specific IC agenda item: "Is this growth rate sustainable enough to justify the premium?"

2. Competitive Validation Requirement

Before passing on competitive grounds, require the deal lead to make one customer call to validate the competitive overlap is real. Salient, Lovable, and Rogo were all mischaracterized by sector-level analysis.

3. 6-Month Re-Check for "Too Early" Passes

Create a scheduled re-evaluation for companies passed as "too early" or "market timing." 5 of 15 misses fit this pattern. A lightweight HAL re-scan at 6 months would have caught Cartesia, Luma, and Ema before their breakout rounds.

4. Monthly Pipeline Hygiene

Auto-surface any Affinity record that has been "in evaluation" for 30+ days without a pass/advance decision. Clay was a $1.25B miss because nobody followed up.

5. YC Batch Scanner Calibration

Update the YC Consensus Scanner scoring model to increase weight on: (a) viral/developer adoption signals, (b) inference cost sensitivity (companies that get better as models get cheaper), (c) founder-market fit for domain experts without startup experience.

6. Thesis-Adjacent Deal Escalation

When Base10 has an active thesis in a vertical (e.g., legal AI via Lexroom), any deal in that vertical should automatically escalate to IC review regardless of valuation. Missing Harvey while investing in Lexroom is a process gap, not a judgment call.
